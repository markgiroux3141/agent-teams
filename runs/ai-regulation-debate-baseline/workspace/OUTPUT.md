## Proposition
"Zero regulation on A.I. so we can get to ASI as soon as possible."

## Summary
The debate centers on whether regulatory constraints on AI development help or harm humanity's path toward artificial superintelligence (ASI). Both sides agree ASI is a consequential endpoint; they differ radically on whether speed or safeguards should be prioritized.

## Strongest FOR Arguments
- **Regulatory delay enables competitors:** OpenAI and Google exploit "safety" framing to consolidate dominance while regulators deliberate; absent domestic regulation, competitors (e.g., Chinese labs) race ahead unencumbered. Speed is a prerequisite for winning a race.
- **Oversight is the bottleneck:** Regulatory frameworks slow iteration and deployment. Every month of compliance dithering is a month closer to competitive disadvantage. In existential competitions, the first to the finish line, not the most cautious, determines the outcome.

## Strongest AGAINST Arguments
- **Speed toward misalignment is faster extinction:** Removing safeguards doesn't accelerate wise AI development—it guarantees the reckless lab wins, not the competent one. Brakes don't stop driving; they enable it safely. Without coordination, the incentive structure selects for corner-cutting.
- **Regulation enables coordination:** Incumbent profit motives may resist regulation, but smart regulatory frameworks can align competitive incentives toward safety rather than recklessness. The "race to the bottom" is a coordination problem; zero regulation doesn't solve it—it surrenders to it.

## Balanced View
Both sides accept that ASI is consequential and that incentive structures matter. They diverge on whether regulation is a neutral cost that slows everyone equally (FOR) or a tool that shapes *how* development occurs (AGAINST). The FOR side prioritizes absolute speed, assuming faster arrival = better outcome. The AGAINST side prioritizes outcome quality, assuming reckless speed = extinction. Neither side engages the hard question: *can regulation accelerate safety without crippling speed*, or are they fundamentally opposed? A grounded view would acknowledge that regulatory *design* (not its mere presence) determines whether it's a handbrake or a collision-avoidance system.

## Points of Genuine Agreement & Miscommunication
- Both implicitly agree a "race" dynamic exists and matters.
- Both care whether ASI is "first" (FOR) or "best" (AGAINST)—but assume these are opposed rather than investigating whether they could align.
- They talk past each other on whether regulation is a speed tax vs. a coordination mechanism.
