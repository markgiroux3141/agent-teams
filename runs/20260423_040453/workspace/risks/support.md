## Failure modes

- Unbriefed coupon endpoint behavior creates support blind spot (likelihood: high, impact: high)
  Mechanism: The `/v2/coupons/apply` endpoint is new, added this week after a scramble, and has not been battle-tested. Support team has received no briefing on what user-facing behavior or error messages changed from v1. Starting Sunday evening, users attempt to apply coupon codes and encounter either new error messages or different validation behavior (edge cases, code formats, restrictions). Support agents consult internal docs written for v1, which describe different error flows. Tickets arrive saying "coupon code didn't apply" or "got an error message I don't recognize." Agents don't know if the behavior is expected or a bug, leading to confusion, repeated escalations to engineering, and frustrated users waiting for clarification.
  Early signal: Starting Sunday afternoon/evening, tickets with keywords "coupon," "code," "apply," "invalid," "rejected," or "not working." Volume spike on these keywords vs. historical baseline. Agents asking in Slack: "Is this a known issue?" or "What changed with coupons?" Help-center article on coupon troubleshooting sees search traffic with users reporting different error messages than the article describes.

- Session re-auth / unexpected logout mid-checkout erodes user trust (likelihood: medium-high, impact: high)
  Mechanism: The new checkout flow may re-authenticate users at different moments than v1 did (the brief flags this as a known concern). A user reaches the payment-entry step and is suddenly logged out, losing all form state (cart, shipping info, partially entered payment details). This has not happened to them before with your checkout, so they interpret it as a system failure. They re-authenticate, restart checkout, and if it happens again, they abandon the purchase and open a ticket blaming the platform. The ticket reads: "Why does your checkout keep logging me out?" implying the product is broken.
  Early signal: Starting Sunday through Monday morning, tickets with keywords "logged out," "session," "signed out," "signed me out," "kicked me out," "mid-checkout," or "unexpectedly." Phrased as frustration: "I was checking out and got logged out." Help-center article "Why was my session signed out?" sees high search traffic with users bouncing back to open tickets because the article describes different behavior. Support team reports seeing this pattern in morning standup.

- Outdated help-center articles amplify confusion and extend ticket resolution time (likelihood: high, impact: medium)
  Mechanism: Help-center articles on "why didn't my coupon apply" and "why was my session signed out" are written for v1 behavior and v1 error messages. On Sunday and Monday, users experience new error messages or flows, search the help center for an explanation, find these articles, read a description that does not match their experience, conclude the article is wrong or out of date, and open a support ticket saying "your help center doesn't match what I'm seeing." Simultaneously, support agents refer to the same articles when responding to tickets, give guidance based on v1 flows ("the error you're describing happens when..."), and that guidance doesn't resolve the user's v2 issue. Tickets take longer to resolve because the frontline knowledge base is stale.
  Early signal: On Monday morning, help-center search volume for "coupon" and "session" terms spikes. Users' initial tickets contain phrases like "I checked your help center article, but my error message is different" or "your article says X happens, but I'm seeing Y." In support Slack, agents report finding articles that don't match user symptoms. Multiple users reference specific article titles in their tickets.

- Weekend staffing bottleneck collides with predictable Monday launch-ramp spike (likelihood: high, impact: high)
  Mechanism: Historical pattern: ticket volume spikes 48–72 hours post-launch, with ~70% confusion tickets (not bugs). Friday ship at start of day → spike begins Sunday midday and extends into Monday. Weekend coverage is 2 agents. If the spike starts Sunday afternoon/evening, those 2 agents begin falling behind. Monday is the 100% ramp day; feature flag goes from 50% to 100% Monday morning. By 10 AM Monday, you have: (1) a pre-built backlog from Sunday, (2) a ramp that doubles user-facing traffic, (3) a skeleton crew until regular team arrives. Response SLA misses, first-response time is hours, users perceive the launch as broken, escalations to management or oncall increase, and the team's morale takes a hit.
  Early signal: Sunday evening (around 48 hours post-ship), support Slack shows 2 agents drowning in queue. Tickets are piling up with response time > 2 hours. By Sunday 11 PM, one or both weekend agents are asking for help or escalating to oncall. Monday 8 AM: ticket queue is 100+, average response time is 4+ hours. Full team arrives to an inherited backlog and angry users.

- Feedback loop lag: support detects bugs first, ops doesn't notice the pattern (likelihood: medium, impact: medium-high)
  Mechanism: The brief notes a "silent degradation" pattern from past launches: tickets are the first signal that something is wrong. For example, if the new coupon endpoint silently fails for certain code formats or cart edge cases, support will receive a flood of "coupon didn't apply" tickets before any monitoring alert fires. Or if the new checkout doesn't handle certain payment methods correctly, support sees "payment failed" tickets first. Without a structured escalation channel to ops, support spends an hour manually determining there's a pattern (5+ similar tickets in 1 hour), then pings ops ad-hoc: "We're seeing a coupon issue." By then, ops has already spent time investigating OTHER alerts, and the root issue was already affecting customers for 60+ minutes. The response is slower, more tickets pile up, and customers perceive silence.
  Early signal: Monday morning, support queue fills with 5+ tickets containing identical keywords (e.g., "coupon invalid," "payment declined," "code not accepted") within a 1–2 hour window. Agents in Slack notice the pattern and ask, "Is this a known issue?" No corresponding alert from ops/monitoring. If support has to manually Slack ops to escalate, you've already lost an hour.

## Mitigations I'd invest in this sprint

- Unbriefed coupon endpoint behavior: Schedule a 30-minute support briefing with the engineer who built the `/v2/coupons/apply` endpoint before Friday launch. Document: (1) specific error codes in v2 vs. v1, (2) user-facing error messages, (3) known validation changes (code format, restrictions, edge cases), (4) any manual tests that revealed unexpected behavior. Create a 1-page internal support guide: "Coupon endpoint v2 — what changed" with a decision tree ("If user says 'code didn't apply,' ask: 1) Did they see an error message? 2) If yes, what did it say?"). Publish before 5 PM Thursday so agents can study Friday morning.

- Session re-auth / unexpected logout: Before Thursday, ask engineering to confirm exactly what re-auth behavior changed between v1 and v2 checkout (session timeout, re-auth on payment entry, re-auth on form submit, etc.). If behavior changed, document it for support. Create a 1-sentence FAQ answer: "Why was I logged out mid-checkout?" with the support response: "If your session is inactive for [X minutes], we re-authenticate for security. Please log back in and your cart will be saved." Brief all 6 support agents on this answer Friday morning. If possible, ask engineering to add a user-facing message in the checkout ("Session expired. Please log in to continue — your cart is saved.") instead of a silent redirect.

- Outdated help-center articles: By end of day Thursday, audit help-center articles for all mentions of "coupon," "session," "logout," and "error." Compare each article's described behavior and error messages against v2 flows. For each article: update to describe v2 behavior, or if the article is mostly v1-specific, add a prominent header: "Updated for our new checkout (April 2026)." For "Why didn't my coupon apply?" specifically, expand the article to list the actual v2 error codes users might see. If audit reveals more than 2–3 articles need revision, prioritize the top 2 and publish the rest as "Known for v2" updates. Do NOT launch with stale articles.

- Weekend staffing / Monday bottleneck: Schedule 4 agents (not 2) for Monday 7 AM – 2 PM to anticipate the 48–72-hour spike and the 100% ramp. Coordinate with ops to set a lower threshold for ops escalation: if 5+ tickets with the same symptom arrive within 1 hour on Monday, support auto-escalates with a message like "Potential issue: 5+ 'coupon didn't apply' tickets in last 60 min." This prepares ops to investigate quickly if there's a real bug.

- Feedback loop lag: Before Friday launch, document a support-to-ops escalation script: "If I see 5+ tickets in 1 hour with the same keyword or error message, I will post in #ops-escalation: '[PATTERN] keyword/symptom, N tickets in last 60 min, first 3 attached.' Ops commits to triaging within 15 minutes." This is a procedural fix, not a tool fix, but it ensures the canary signal reaches ops quickly.
